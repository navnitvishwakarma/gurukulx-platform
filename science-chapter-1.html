Lesson 1: What is Matter?

15 minutes Beginner

Definition of Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space. Everything you can see, touch, smell, or taste is made of matter. This includes:

Examples of Matter:

  • Solids: Books, tables, rocks, ice
  • Liquids: Water, milk, oil, juice
  • Gases: Air, steam, oxygen, carbon dioxide

Characteristics of Matter

All matter has two fundamental characteristics:

Mass

The amount of material in an object. Measured in grams (g) or kilograms (kg).

Volume

The amount of space an object occupies. Measured in cubic centimeters (cm³) or liters (L).

Key Points to Remember:

  • Matter is everywhere around us
  • All matter has mass and volume
  • Matter can exist in different states
  • Even things we can't see (like air) are matter

Lesson 2: States of Matter

20 minutes Beginner

The Three States of Matter

Matter can exist in three main states, depending on temperature and pressure:

Solid

Shape: Definite shape

Volume: Definite volume

Particles: Closely packed, vibrate in place

Examples: Ice, wood, metal

Liquid

Shape: Takes shape of container

Volume: Definite volume

Particles: Loosely packed, can move around

Examples: Water, oil, milk

Gas

Shape: No definite shape

Volume: No definite volume

Particles: Far apart, move freely

Examples: Air, steam, oxygen

Changes of State

Matter can change from one state to another when energy is added or removed:

Solid Melting Liquid
Liquid Evaporation Gas
Gas Condensation Liquid
Liquid Freezing Solid

Lesson 3: Physical Properties

18 minutes Intermediate

What are Physical Properties?

Physical properties are characteristics that can be observed or measured without changing the substance into something else.

Color

The appearance of an object based on the light it reflects

Gold is yellow Copper is reddish

Density

Mass per unit volume (Density = Mass ÷ Volume)

Wood floats on water Iron sinks in water

Melting Point

Temperature at which a solid becomes a liquid

Ice melts at 0°C Gold melts at 1064°C

Boiling Point

Temperature at which a liquid becomes a gas

Water boils at 100°C Alcohol boils at 78°C

Hardness

Resistance to being scratched or dented

Diamond is very hard Talc is very soft

Solubility

Ability to dissolve in a solvent

Salt dissolves in water Oil doesn't dissolve in water

Lesson 4: Chemical Properties

20 minutes Intermediate

What are Chemical Properties?

Chemical properties describe how a substance reacts with other substances to form new materials.

Flammability

The ability of a substance to burn or catch fire

Paper Highly flammable
Water Not flammable

Reactivity

How easily a substance reacts with other substances

Sodium Very reactive with water
Gold Very unreactive

Oxidation

Reaction with oxygen, often causing rust or tarnish

Iron Rusts in moist air
Silver Tarnishes over time

Hands-on Experiments

Experiment 1: States of Water

Materials Needed:

  • Ice cubes
  • Water
  • Heat source (stove or hot plate)
  • Thermometer

Procedure:

  1. Observe the ice cubes (solid state)
  2. Place ice in a container and let it melt (solid to liquid)
  3. Heat the water until it boils (liquid to gas)
  4. Record the temperatures at each change

What to Observe:

Notice how the particles behave differently in each state and how energy affects the changes.

Experiment 2: Density Test

Materials Needed:

  • Water
  • Oil
  • Small objects (cork, coin, wood)
  • Clear container

Procedure:

  1. Fill container halfway with water
  2. Add oil slowly
  3. Drop different objects into the container
  4. Observe which objects float and which sink

What to Observe:

Objects with higher density than water will sink, while those with lower density will float.
