Lesson 1: Properties of Light

18 minutes Beginner

What is Light?

Light is a form of energy that enables us to see objects. It travels in straight lines and at a very high speed.

Key Properties of Light:

  • Speed: Light travels at approximately 300,000 km/s in vacuum
  • Travel: Light travels in straight lines
  • Reflection: Light bounces off surfaces
  • Refraction: Light bends when passing through different materials

Sources of Light

Natural Sources

  • Sun
  • Stars
  • Fireflies
  • Lightning

Artificial Sources

  • Electric bulbs
  • LED lights
  • Candles
  • Torches

How We See Objects

1

Light Source

Light is emitted from a source (like the sun or a bulb)

2

Object Illumination

Light falls on objects and gets reflected

3

Eye Reception

Reflected light enters our eyes

4

Brain Processing

Our brain processes the light signals to form images

Lesson 2: Reflection and Refraction

22 minutes Intermediate

Reflection of Light

Reflection occurs when light bounces off a surface. There are two types of reflection:

Regular Reflection

Light reflects off smooth surfaces like mirrors, creating clear images

Examples: Mirrors, still water, polished metal

Diffuse Reflection

Light reflects off rough surfaces in many directions, creating scattered light

Examples: Paper, walls, most everyday objects

Laws of Reflection

First Law

The incident ray, reflected ray, and normal all lie in the same plane

Second Law

The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection

Refraction of Light

Refraction occurs when light passes from one medium to another and changes direction.

Why Does Refraction Happen?

Light travels at different speeds in different materials. When it enters a new medium, it bends due to this speed change.

Pencil in Water: Appears bent when viewed from the side
Rainbow: Formed by refraction and dispersion of sunlight in water droplets
Lenses: Use refraction to focus or spread light

Lesson 3: Sound Waves

20 minutes Intermediate

What is Sound?

Sound is a form of energy that travels as waves through air, water, or other materials. It is produced by vibrations.

Frequency

Number of vibrations per second, measured in Hertz (Hz)

High frequency = High pitch Low frequency = Low pitch

Amplitude

Height of the sound wave, determines volume

Large amplitude = Loud sound Small amplitude = Soft sound

Wavelength

Distance between two consecutive wave peaks

Short wavelength = High frequency Long wavelength = Low frequency

How Sound Travels

Vibration

An object vibrates, creating sound waves

Wave Propagation

Sound waves travel through air as compressions and rarefactions

Reception

Our ears detect the sound waves and convert them to electrical signals

Speed of Sound

Sound travels at different speeds in different materials:

  • Air: 343 m/s (at 20°C)
  • Water: 1,500 m/s
  • Steel: 5,000 m/s
  • Vacuum: 0 m/s (sound cannot travel in vacuum)

Lesson 4: Echo and Reverberation

15 minutes Beginner

What is an Echo?

An echo is the reflection of sound waves off a surface. We hear an echo when the reflected sound reaches our ears after the original sound.

Conditions for Hearing Echo:

  • The reflecting surface must be at least 17 meters away
  • The surface should be hard and smooth
  • There should be no obstacles between the source and the reflecting surface
  • The original sound should be loud enough

Reverberation

Reverberation occurs when sound waves reflect multiple times off different surfaces, creating a prolonged sound effect.

Good Reverberation

Enhances music and speech in concert halls and theaters

Concert halls Churches Theaters

Excessive Reverberation

Makes speech unclear and music muddy

Empty rooms Tunnels Caves

Sound Absorption

Some materials absorb sound waves instead of reflecting them, reducing echoes and reverberation.

Good Absorbers

  • Carpets and rugs
  • Curtains and drapes
  • Foam padding
  • Fiberglass insulation

Poor Absorbers

  • Glass windows
  • Bare walls
  • Hard floors
  • Metal surfaces

Lesson 5: Light and Sound in Daily Life

16 minutes Beginner

Applications of Light

Vision

Light enables us to see the world around us

Photography

Cameras capture light to create images

Illumination

Artificial lights help us see in the dark

Lenses

Glasses and microscopes use light refraction

Applications of Sound

Communication

Speech and music are forms of sound communication

Sonar

Uses sound waves to detect objects underwater

Medical Imaging

Ultrasound uses sound waves to create images of the body

Alarms

Sound alerts warn us of danger or important events

Safety and Health

Light Safety

  • Never look directly at the sun
  • Use proper eye protection when welding
  • Take breaks from bright screens
  • Use appropriate lighting for reading

Sound Safety

  • Wear ear protection in loud environments
  • Keep music volume at safe levels
  • Take breaks from loud sounds
  • Be aware of noise pollution

Hands-on Experiments

Experiment 1: Making a Rainbow

Materials Needed:

  • Glass of water
  • White paper
  • Sunlight or flashlight
  • Prism (optional)

Procedure:

  1. Fill a glass with water
  2. Place it near a window with sunlight
  3. Hold white paper behind the glass
  4. Adjust the angle to see a rainbow on the paper

What to Observe:

Notice how white light is separated into different colors due to refraction and dispersion.

Experiment 2: Sound Vibrations

Materials Needed:

  • Empty can
  • Balloon
  • Rubber band
  • Small pieces of paper

Procedure:

  1. Cut the balloon and stretch it over the can opening
  2. Secure with a rubber band
  3. Place small paper pieces on the balloon
  4. Make sounds near the can and observe the paper pieces

What to Observe:

Watch how the paper pieces move when sound waves hit the balloon, showing that sound is made of vibrations.
